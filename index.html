Chemistry

There are 5 states of matter if you heat each state it will become the next state

  1. BEO(Bose Einstein Condensate): gas frozen to near 0 Kelvin
  2. Solid: the forces keeping the particles together are relatively strong and particles stay very close together so they are hard and rigid
  3. Liquid: the forces that hold the particles together are weaker than in solids they can flow around in a container
  4. Gases: often invisible but everywhere the forces are weaker than solid or liquid you can sense them though some are lethal and some are for helping humans
    • Elemental Gases – gases that consist of one Element(listed below)
    • Diatomic Gases – the basic unit is a molecule of two atoms joined together
    • Monatomic Gases – the basic unit is one atom
    • Compound Gases – made from more than one element

    • Kinetic Molecular Theory of Gases
        img-chem-5 pressure in a container
      1. Molecules are always moving in a straight line until they collide with other or into walls
      2. Energy is not lost when a particle collides in the molecule world – elastic collision
        In the human world collisions lose energy because of friction – inelastic collision
      3. a single particle of gas is nothing compared to the space it takes as a gas it take such little space that it isn't considered as matter
      4. the particles of gas don't attract each other due to the fact that they are so far away from each other

      This explains pressure, the force of the collisions of lots of molecules hitting the walls is called pressure.
      if the pressure has too much stress on the container it will cause an explosion which is a rapid expantion of gas as shown in img-chem-5


      All of a gas's energy is in the form of kinetic energy(energy from movement).
      According to the Kinetic Molecular Theory, molecules do not lose energy when they collide, this means the usual kinetic energy of the molecules stays the same.
      Any change in kinetic energy is followed by a change in temperature and when there is a change in the temperature, there is a change in the kinetic energy.

      • Units for Measuring Gases

      • Volume – Capacity Units(amount of space a gas takes)
        • mL(millilitres)
        • L(litres)
        • small amounts – µL(microlitres)
        • large amounts – kL(kilolitres)

    • Temperature
      • Celsius Scale
        • Named after a Swedish astronomer – Anders Celsius
        • Used in day-to-day life for temperature
        • In the metric system
        • Originaly based off water because people wanted to know when water changed state(from solid to liquid ext.) so it works for liquids but not gas

      • Fahrenheit scale
        • In the imperial system
        • Originaly based off water because people wanted to know when water changed state(from solid to liquid ext.) so it works for liquids but not gas

        The reason these two scales don't work is because kinetic energy is related to temperature and is something is at 0℃ or 0℉ that dosen't mean it has no kinetic energy

      • So there is a unit of mesurement in which 0 means no kinetic energy(no movement between particles): K(Kelvin or absolute temprature scale).
        • Named after Lord Kelvin of England
        • 0 kelvin(absolute zero) is the lowest temprature
        • No degree sign so the freezing point of water is 0℃ or 273.15 K
        • Add 273.15 to Celsius to get Kelvin equivilent
      • In 1970's the metric system was used in Canada for scientific research
